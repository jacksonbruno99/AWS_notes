Day 1 – AWS Global Infrastructure

Regions

  • A Region is a geographical area containing two or more Availability Zones (AZs).
  • Regions are isolated — no automatic replication between them unless configured.
  • Impact of Region choice: latency, cost, data residency, service availability.
  • Not all services are available in every region — always check AWS Regional Services List.

Availability Zones (AZs)

  • An AZ = one or more physically separate data centers with independent power, cooling, networking.
  • AZs within a Region are connected via low-latency links (1–2 ms).
  • Best practice: Deploy EC2, RDS, and load balancers across at least two AZs for HA.
  • Fault isolation — one AZ failure does not affect others.

Edge Locations

  • Used by CloudFront, Global Accelerator, and Route 53.
  • Global cache nodes bring content closer to users.
  • Edge = content delivery, not compute.

Local & Wavelength Zones

  • Local Zones extend AWS services closer to metro areas — reduces latency.
  • Wavelength Zones for 5G ultra-low-latency apps.

Fault Tolerance Concepts

  • Multi-AZ deployments survive single AZ failure.
  • Multi-Region deployment for disaster recovery and global resilience.
  • S3 & DynamoDB are multi-AZ by default; EC2, RDS, EBS are single-AZ unless configured.

Networking & Latency Insight

  • Cross-AZ traffic within a region is free; cross-region traffic incurs charges.
  • Keep data close to users to minimize latency and cost.

Global Services

  • Some services are global: IAM, Route 53, CloudFront, WAF, AWS Organizations.
  • IAM is global — no region selection needed.
  • Exam tip: “Is IAM a regional or global service?” → Global.
